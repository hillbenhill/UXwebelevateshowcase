1 What The Product Is

Product Name - Haven

Haven Concept Overview

With the rapid population ageing that is occurring world-wide, there is increasing interest in “smart home” technologies that can assist older adults to continue living at home with safety and independence.

Improved health and social care over recent years has increased life expectancy worldwide. As a result, in 2010 an estimated 524 million people were aged 65 or older - 8% of the world’s. By 2050, this number is expected to nearly triple to about 1.5 billion, representing 16% of the world’s population.

The current trends in population demographics make it increasingly difficult for governments worldwide to fully support the health and social care systems. Therefore, a large proportion of older adults rely solely on family members for assisted care, often resulting in great emotional stress for family caregivers.

As Diane Cook, lead researcher of Washington State University’s “Smart Home Projects” describes, “Individuals don’t want to leave their home even if they live in a rural area and don’t have easy access to healthcare and family members. They still will not leave their family home. Studies show that when they do – particularly against their will – that move is often followed by cognitive and physical decline and death”.

Smart-homes are purpose designed living spaces that provide interactive technologies and unobtrusive support systems to enable people to enjoy a higher level of independence, activity, participation or well-being than would otherwise be available. With these home based technologies there is potential for a person to live in their own home without the need to be hospitalised or institutionalised. Empowering the elderly to live independently instead of in an adult care facility not only provides financial savings, it also has the potential to improve quality of life, reduce the strain on aged care facilities, and provide relief for family caregivers.

However, we need a more viable and scalable solution to meet this demand. Enter a new wave of smart home technology known as “assistive domotics”.

Assistive domotics is an application of home automation that focuses on enabling elderly or disabled people to live at their home instead of a nursing home or similar healthcare facility. These products use sensors (eg: Bluetooth) to track movements in the home, or lack thereof, of the relevant resident. Sensors can be placed on any item, including doors, keychains, and even pillboxes. GPS technology documents precise location information of the person, logs activity, and reports it to a family member living somewhere else. Haven aims to harness all these available features and more. By using assistive domotics, some of the principal benefits Haven could provide to help independent living for the elderly are:

  • Emergency Response
  • Eyesight and hearing
  • Memory
  • Video/Audio monitoring
  • Medication Assistance

In addition to these immediate benefits to the resident, this type of technology provides support to those in the “sandwich generation,” a term coined for those simultaneously supporting aging parents and their children.

Product Definition

Haven is aimed at family, carers and medical professionals of the elderly or disabled people who wish to live in their home safely and independently for as long as possible.
By utilising assistive domotics such as wearable tech and sensors, Haven will record an individual’s body readings and track their activities inside and outside the house.
The carers will be able to unobtrusively follow a person’s daily routine by reading simple time line charts accessible via website or downloadable applications for tablets and smartphones.
Currently we believe there is a lack of appropriate applications for tablets, smartphones and systems which avail of smart home technology to monitor and care for the vulnerable who wish to remain in their home.

Assistive Domotics: A form of home automation which focuses on making it possible for older adults and people with disabilities to remain at home, safe and comfortable.
Futher development is needed in this area to help address the currrent burden on under resourced health services and gowth in ageing populations.

2 Who Will Use It

Online of User Surveys
Haven is centered around vulnerable residents but is aimed at the care support system (family members, carers and medical professionals).
Therefore online surveys and interviews where devised to focus on these two groups: Residents and Carers.
Majority of surveyed carers are between 18-24 (22.2%) and 35-44 years old (27.8%).
Over half of them where family members (61.1%). Most of them ( 55.6%) are in contact with residents on a daily if not hourly basis.
The most common type of care provided was of personal assistance - getting out of bed, bathing, using the toilet, dressing, walking or eating (72.2%) and Dementia / Alzheimer's Care (44.4%).

When asked to choose the most important of Haven’s core functions, the highest scoring was Alarms & Notifications, followed closely by Vital body readings (eg. blood pressure, sugar level), the Calendar, which is shared by carers, family, medical staff and the Care Network.
The only function that was deemed less important was Activity charts (eg. shows activity and indicates movement around various areas of the house). None were voted unimportant.

Persona

  • Name: Margaret Gallagher
  • Age: 57
  • Education: Degree in Business Studies
  • Occupation: Finance Manager
  • Interests: Cooking, TV dramas and fundraising

Personal details:
Margaret is a single mother with two adult children who live a few time zones away.
She lives a short distance from her elderly father’s home. She is also working as a Finance Manager in a local company, but she still takes flexi time to check on her father, Anthony, and to prepare his meals.
She is quite busy at work and is not yet eligible for an early retirement.
A home help person is employed to visit three times a week to do some housework for her father.

Anthony has been widowed for nearly 2 years during which time it has been difficult for him to cope.
Recently he has started wandering late at night and just last month he nearly got knocked down by a car.
He is quite deaf, so didn’t hear the car coming. This clearly distressed Margaret and she is looking for some form of security system through which she can keep an eye on her dad in the evenings.

Skills:
Margaret has a Windows PC at work, and has a Windows-based laptop for the past few years.
She’s thinking of upgrading to a bigger smartphone as she finds it difficult to work with small touchscreens.
Being a finance manager she is accustomed to working with numbers and spreadsheets - she is meticulous and a firm believer in doing things “by the book”.

Goals:
Keeping track of her father’s well being and location in the evenings. Having a call service to fall back on when she or the home help are not around in case of emergencies.

Where will Margret use Haven: Anytime she looks on her phone she will see if there is a notification.
She will be able to check on Anthony wellness charts from work where she has internet access.

When will Margret use Haven: She’ll be able to access the data 24/7.
Most frequently it will be among the first things on her phone to check, and in the evening during her chat with her father.

What is Margaret’s attitude regarding Haven:
Margaret has heard of Haven from her niece Jenny who works as a care assistant in a local daycare centre.
Jenny mentioned that one of their regular visitors with dementia wears a smart bracelet that monitors his sugar and blood pressure.
It also helps the family to monitor their elderly relative in case he wanders off on his own.

Margaret looks up the service and books an appointment. The Haven agent brings a mini kit to demonstrate the working and principle behind the bracelet and other parts of the system.
She understands that the camera and the microphone are activated by a call centre carer only when there is no notification confirmation from the list of client authorised first responders.
She has a couple of queries, namely whether the system would be secure and also does it require an internet connection in order to operate.

The agent explains that the sensor system within the house feeds the data to a router which doesn't need an internet connection to operate.
However in order to set up Haven and to be able to receive regular readings of daily activity charts, she would need WiFi or mobile internet on her own devices.
The bracelet, she is told, has its own tracking device that doesn't require internet either.

Margaret decides to have a discussion with Anthony in order to see how he would feel about it. Although at first, he is apprehensive about it, after a bit of explaining they decide to use the system for a two week trial.
Next month Margaret will be busy with quarterly revenue reports at work, so she is keen to see if this system works for her father and herself.

Within the first week of the trial, Margaret pinpoints why her father tends to wake so often at night: he has problem with constipation which makes him feel unsettled.
He would go to the bathroom but then get disoriented in the dark and leave the house instead.
During this product trial, Margaret has connected her father's GP and the home help person to the Haven network, and all three of them now have a better insight of how well or unwell Anthony is feeling.

Margaret Now she is receiving notifications if her father is feeling restless; Anthony’s GP has recommended dietary adjustments via the network, based on Anthony’s toilet visits and the carer knows if he has had a meal or not.

On which device:
Bracelet, android smartphone and laptop.

Persona

  • Name: Eva Clarke
  • Age: 52
  • Education: BSc in General Nursing
  • Occupation: District Nurse
  • Interests: Scout leading and carol singing

Personal details:
Eva is single and has no children. Working for the HSE as a district nurse keeps her busy. Her patients are spread out over a 15 km radius.
She visits 7-8 of them daily to ensure they are supported and assisted in changing bandages and administering injections and pumps.
Sometimes emotions can run high in households when the family members are experiencing difficulty coping with the changes to their loved ones.
Whilst leaving a patient Eva is often distressed as she knows that these families are not sufficiently familiar with emergency procedures that could help them cope. She is the first line of support for her at-home patients and has to constantly be alert when on call.


Skills:
Eva works on an old PC at the clinic, so her Office knowledge is not entirely up to date. She uses a smartphone regularly, as well as an e-reader which she finds handy for keeping up with her nursing courses.
A big fan of Facebook, she likes to keep in touch with her sisters who live in the United States.

Goals:
Quicker assessment of situation during emergency calls.
Ability to follow up on patients remotely and understanding the patient's needs better.

What is Eva’s attitude regarding Haven:
While visiting a neighbour - a Type 2 diabetes patient called Jim, she discovers he has developed a severe infection in one of his toes.
This happened due to Jim’s phobia of hospitals. Eva’s been contacted only after Jim’s grandson had found him on the floor trying to get up.
While looking after Jim, his grandson Mark approaches Eva inquiring about importance of body readings.
Intrigued Eva hears about Haven and agrees to participate in Jim and Mark’s trial of the system. She joins them on the day they have the agent over to configure the smart bracelet for Jim.
She is instructed on how to enter his baseline information, access the information from the Haven app on her smartphone and observe the changes in Jim’s vital signs; print out charts and share insights with family via the care network.

The trial takes two weeks.
Eva finds the system very helpful - in fact helping her ascertain the time when Jim was having a particularly bad episode of tachycardia. He had complained about a lot of fatigue lately, so Eva set the alarm for extreme values of blood pressure specific to Jim.
A deviation occurred and she was immediately sent a notification from Haven while she was on her way to the clinic: Jim’s blood pressure was unusually low and his pulse rate was rather high.
She was able to return as soon as possible and help Jim avoid potential heart failure.

After this incident, she has spoken to her colleagues and doctors at the clinic about Haven.
Some are considering offering the smart bracelets to their patients who would prefer to stay at home with remote monitoring which involves the carers and family. However, some colleagues believe this may increase their workload and build a patient's expectancy to have the medical staff available on call.

Where will Margret use Haven:
At work and off work in case of emergency notifications.

When will Margret use Haven:
During her work hours. She has agreed to keep an eye on Jim’s case and is receiving alert notifications for emergencies.

On which device:
On Eva’s Android smartphone

Margaret’s scenario:

Why did we pick the scenarios we did?

Illustrates the use of the wearable device.
Persona achieves the goal of being up to date with her father’s well being and having a backup call service when she or the home help are not available in the case of emergencies.

It’s almost lunchtime and Margaret is at work. She has just finished her weekly financial meeting with management.
She returns to her office and notices there are some notifications on her smartphone from the care system installed in her father’s home. It is informing her that there are spikes in his vitals reports.
This lets her know that her father is feeling unwell.
Just then her phone rings and it’s an emergency call from the Haven system.
Because there was a delay in Margaret responding to the messages, the system’s head office has been automatically notified and a call was made instead to Margaret, the care recipient’s primary carer.
When at work Margaret is a thirty minute drive to her father’s home so before making this journey she is able to log onto the care system on her phone and activate the cameras installed throughout his home in case of emergencies.
This will allow her to assess the situation and decide if she needs to call the emergency services. She is able to see her father in his bedroom.
He appears to be responsive, She calls him and from their conversation she establishes that he has shortness of breath and is feeling lethargic.
She immediately calls his doctor and arranges to meet him at her father’s house.
She cancels her work scheduled for the afternoon and leaves work to be with her father.

Mary's' Scenario

Mary the community nurse – part of her regular rounds it looking after some of the residents living in sheltered accommodation.
When on duty she logs into the patient monitoring dashboard and is updated with the health screening charts of her patients. One of the elderly residents has diabetes, hypertension and arrhythmia.
Upon login she is able pull up the patient's chart for the past week.
If consistent readings are outside of normal, an alert is generated and flagged with the current logged in user.
Mary gets an alert and can see that the patient in question is showing very high blood pressure and poor control of blood glucose over the last 48-72 hours. She moves the case review into her high priority todo list for her daily rounds.
Upon seeing the patient she is able to take a blood pressure reading and review the level of compliance with the diabetes intervention regime.
Mary updates the patient’s data on the system and marks the task off as done.
With the data and the physical review Mary is able to make a judgment.
Follow up with a doctor’s visit to investigate further or call in the emergency services.
The Gp will be sent an alert as part of the community team, assigned a follow up call and view supporting data on the system.
The system enables the community nursing team prioritise patient care based on a dynamic personalised need basis.

Use Case

List of Actors

The HAVEN system is complex but it boils down to these three actors:
  • Mobile Application User
  • Server
  • Call Center
  • Resident and home sensors

List of Use Cases

These have been chosen in order to illustrate the key functionality that sets this service appart from it’s competitors.
    For App user:
  • CHECK REPORTS
  • SET THE SETTINGS PARAMETERS
  • START VIDEO CALL
  • EMAIL A REPORT
    For server the chosen use cases are:
  • RUN CHECKS ON NEW DATA
  • SEND ALARM TO THE USER APP
  • SEND ALARM TO THE CALL CENTER
  • SEND SMS TO USERS PHONE (bad day)
    Call center:
  • CHECK ON THE RESIDENT

Use Case Diagrams

Settings function will be opening screen the very first time application is run on mobile device.
After completion, user can continue to Main Menu.
After the first intial setup the Login function will be the default starting point.
This is to hide the change the settings function form the user, as a calibrating the personal sensors is a process which requires a qualified technician during the set up.
Upon choosing the configuration menu, user sets up the needed sensors by setting the baseline values range.
Any data outside of this rage will prompt the Haven server to send alarms to the App User ( reference good day, bad day).
While configuring the settings, user will be indirectly interacting with wearable device on the resident, testing for the accuracy of the readings.
Before committing to the configuration user reviews the settings.
